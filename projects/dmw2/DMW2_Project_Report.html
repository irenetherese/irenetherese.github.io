ABSTRACT¶

In the rapidly evolving landscape of digital entertainment, the ability to provide tailored game recommendations has become a pivotal asset for enhancing user engagement and satisfaction. This study addresses the challenge of leveraging user preferences and game similarities to offer precise game recommendations. Our research embarked on developing two distinct recommender systems: a unary aggregation-based system and a rules-based system, each designed to analyze and predict game titles that users would likely enjoy, based on their past interactions and preferences.

Our methodology encompassed a comprehensive process starting with meticulous data gathering and preprocessing, which included exploratory data analysis, filtering of reviews for quality, and the elimination of null values to ensure the integrity of our dataset. Subsequently, we focused on constructing detailed user and item profiles through careful feature selection, laying the groundwork for our recommender systems.

Upon building the recommender systems, we proceeded with a rigorous validation phase, employing cosine similarity metrics to benchmark our recommenders against a global baseline and Steam's own recommendation algorithms. Through random sampling, we evaluated the effectiveness and accuracy of our recommendations.

The findings from our study revealed that both the unary aggregation and rules-based recommender systems significantly prioritize game content similarities, diverging from conventional approaches that often rely heavily on popularity or trending metrics. This emphasis on content affinities over mere popularity enables a more personalized recommendation experience, particularly benefiting streamers and content creators who seek to provide unique and tailored content to their audiences. Moreover, our approach democratizes exposure for indie games, offering them equal footing in the recommendation process and thus fostering a richer diversity within the gaming and streaming ecosystem.

In conclusion, our study demonstrates the potential of leveraging sophisticated recommender systems to enhance game discovery and personalization, presenting a valuable tool for streamers, gamers, and the broader gaming community to uncover games that resonate with their individual tastes and preferences.

PROBLEM STATEMENT¶

In the diverse and ever-expanding universe of digital gaming, both gamers and streamers are constantly faced with an overwhelming choice of titles, making the decision of what game to play next a daunting challenge. This selection process is further complicated by the dynamic nature of gaming trends and the vast array of individual preferences.

Our study is anchored by the critical question: How can we leverage user preferences and game similarities for effective game recommendations?

This problem statement underscores the necessity for a nuanced approach to recommendation systems, one that not only navigates through the sheer volume of available games but also aligns closely with the unique tastes and interests of each user. The aim is to transcend traditional recommendation methods, which often prioritize popularity and trends, to develop a system that champions content relevance and personalization, thereby enhancing the gaming experience for users and providing streamers with a toolkit to engage their audience with content that resonates.

DATA SOURCE¶

For our study, we leveraged two distinct datasets that provide a comprehensive view of the gaming landscape on Steam. These datasets are pivotal for developing our recommender systems, as they offer a deep dive into both the technical aspects and community feedback of thousands of games.

Steam Games Dataset

Our primary dataset is a detailed compilation of data on Steam games, obtained from Kaggle. It was meticulously assembled using a custom code (licensed under MIT) that taps into the Steam API and incorporates data from Steam Spy, a reputable third-party service known for its estimates on game sales and user engagement metrics. This dataset encompasses a wide array of information about Steam games, including technical specifications, user engagement metrics, and content descriptions, with a total of 85,103 data points across 39 features.

Source: This dataset was aggregated through a custom-built code that interacts with the Steam API and Steam Spy to fetch a wealth of information on each game. This includes details on estimated ownership, peak concurrent users, pricing, and user reviews. The structured data is then compiled into a CSV file, which has been made available on Kaggle for research purposes.

Collection Process: The custom code queries the Steam API and Steam Spy for comprehensive details on each listed game, capturing essential metrics that offer insights into player engagement and preferences.

Update Frequency: To ensure relevance and accuracy, the dataset undergoes monthly updates. The most recent update, as of this writing, was in January 2024.

Steam Reviews Dataset

The second dataset in our study encompasses a comprehensive collection of user reviews for video games on the Steam Store. This dataset is rich with details about each review, offering insights beyond mere textual feedback. Key information includes the steam ID, app ID (identifying the reviewed game), playtime (indicating how long the reviewer has played the game), the review text itself, a binary indicator of like/dislike, and timestamps marking when the review was created and last updated. This data was meticulously scraped from the Steam API. It holds rating data from 6M unique users, with 15M reviews over 8,183 games.

By including a diverse set of parameters such as playtime and like/dislike indicators, this dataset offers a multi-dimensional view of user engagement and satisfaction. This dataset is instrumental in our endeavor to develop a recommender system that not only aligns with user preferences based on content similarities but also takes into account the nuanced perspectives of the gaming community.

This detailed compilation of user reviews significantly enriches our research, enabling a deeper understanding of player experiences and preferences. It serves as a critical foundation for tailoring our recommender systems to better match gamers with titles that resonate with their individual tastes and gameplay histories.

def calculate_cosine_similarity(rated_profiles, recommended_profiles):
    similarity_matrix = cosine_similarity(rated_profiles.values, recommended_profiles.values)
    mean_similarity_score = similarity_matrix.mean()
    return mean_similarity_score


def compute_user_profile_agg_unary(df_utility, df_item_profiles, user):
    """Compute the user profile by unary aggregation."""
    user_ratings = df_utility.loc[user]
    mean = user_ratings.mean()
    user_ratings = user_ratings.apply(lambda x: 1 if x >= mean else 0)

    nan_mask = np.isnan(user_ratings)
    weighted_profiles = (user_ratings.values.reshape(-1, 1)
                         * df_item_profiles.values)

    weighted_profiles[nan_mask] = 0
    user_profiles = (weighted_profiles.sum(axis=0)
                     / user_ratings.mask(nan_mask).sum())

    return user_profiles


def recommend_agg_unary(df_utility, df_item_profiles, user_profile, user, L):
    """Recommend L items to the user based on unary aggregation method."""
    similarities = cosine_similarity([user_profile], df_item_profiles)
    sorted_indices = np.argsort(similarities[0])[::-1]

    recommended_items = []
    seen_items = set(df_utility.loc[user].dropna().index)
    for idx in sorted_indices:
        item_id = df_item_profiles.iloc[idx].name
        if item_id not in seen_items:
            recommended_items.append(item_id)
            if len(recommended_items) == L:
                break

    return recommended_items

def find_rules_unary(df_utility, df_item_profiles, user, minsup, minconf):
    """Compute the user profile using rules."""
    rated_items = df_utility.loc[user].dropna().index.tolist()

    transactions = pd.Series(index=rated_items, dtype=object)
    for item in rated_items:
        t_features = (df_item_profiles.loc[item]
                      [df_item_profiles.loc[item] > 0]
                      .index.tolist())
        transactions[item] = t_features

    patterns = fim.fpgrowth(transactions.values, supp=minsup)

    rules = []
    for pattern, support in patterns:
        features = sorted(list(set(pattern)))
        indices = [item for item, item_features in transactions.items()
                   if set(features).issubset(item_features)]
        ratings = ['LIKE' if rating > 0 else None for rating
                   in df_utility.loc[user][indices]]
        ratings_dict = {}
        for item in ratings:
            if item in ratings_dict:
                ratings_dict[item] += 1
            else:
                ratings_dict[item] = 1
        rating = max(ratings_dict, key=lambda x: ratings_dict[x])
        confidence = (ratings_dict[rating] / len(indices)) * 100
        if features and rating and confidence >= minconf:
            new_rule = {
                'rating': rating,
                'features': features,
                'confidence': confidence}
            rules.append(new_rule)

    rules.sort(key=lambda x: (-x['confidence'], x['features']))

    return rules


def recommend_rules(df_utility, df_item_profiles, user_profile, user, L):
    """Recommend L items to the user based on the rules method."""
    item_scores = {}
    rated_items = set(df_utility.loc[user].dropna().index)
    for item_id, item_profile in df_item_profiles.iterrows():
        if item_id in rated_items:
            continue

        score = 0
        features = (df_item_profiles.loc[item_id]
                    [df_item_profiles.loc[item_id] > 0]
                    .index.tolist())

        for rule in user_profile:
            if set(rule['features']).issubset(features):
                if rule['rating'] == 'LIKE':
                    score += (rule['confidence'])
                elif rule['rating'] == 'DISLIKE':
                    score -= (rule['confidence'])
        item_scores[item_id] = score

    sorted_items = sorted(item_scores.items(), key=lambda x: (-x[1], x[0]))
    recommended_items = [item[0] for item in sorted_items[:L]]

    return recommended_items

These steps are executed iteratively for varying minimum support values, producing a set of results including the minimum support, similarity score, and runtime for each evaluation.

Through this iterative process, we seek to optimize the recommendation system by fine-tuning the minimum support parameter. This parameter plays a crucial role in association rule mining and affects the quality of recommendations. SUMMARY AND INSIGHTS¶

  • Our recommender prioritizes game content similarities over popularity or trends, assisting game discovery tailored to preferences in terms of content.

  • This approach is beneficial for streamers who prioritize tailored content creation, offering a better fit for their needs.

  • Since popularity isn't a primary factor, it also offers indie games equal exposure opportunities for discovery within the streaming community, thus enhancing diversity.

RECOMMENDATION¶

To improve the effectiveness and personalization of recommendation systems for projects, such as those involving game recommendations, a strategic approach focusing on both personalization and performance is necessary. Below is a recommended plan based on the outlined objectives:

Improving Personalization of Recommenders Personalization is key to engaging users by providing recommendations that closely match their interests and preferences. Here's how to improve it:

  • Weighted Feature Engineering: Incorporates weighted features, prioritizing elements that significantly impact user preferences. This could involve:

  • Genre and Category Analysis: Assign higher weights to genres or categories that users show a consistent preference for.

  • Date Range Checks and Release Dates: Incorporate features that consider how recent a game is and whether it falls within a user's preferred date range, adjusting the weight based on these factors.

  • Popularity Filters: Implement filters based on game's popularity.

Improving Performance of Recommenders Enhancing the performance of recommendation systems involves optimizing their efficiency, accuracy, and scalability. Here are the ways to achieve this:

  • Apply data reduction techniques on Game Profiles.

  • Apply clustering on game profiles

  • Extending the data by aggregating to other datasets for game profiles like Twitch and data from other platforms such as GoG and Epic.
